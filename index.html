Rose Symbollism and Color

The rose, a timeless and beloved flower, holds many meanings and much symbolism across different countries and cultures.

With their beauty, versatility and classic shape, it’s hardly surprising that roses are such a popular choice for special occasions like anniversaries, birthdays, Mother's Day and (of course) Valentine's Day.

Here at Floraly, we have a wide range of fresh rose bouquets to help you find the perfect gift for those special moments in life.

Over the years, roses have come to symbolise some of our strongest feelings, such as love, passion and admiration. Whether you want to express love, friendship, or joy, there’s a rose for every emotion and occasion.

Not only do roses symbolise different things depending on their colour, their meanings can also vary between cultures. Even the number of roses can express different meanings! If you love roses but aren’t sure which ones to pick, you’re in the right place. Read on to learn about the different meanings and symbolism of roses.

ROSE SYMBOLISM THROUGHOUT THE AGES

Humanity’s love affair with roses can be traced back to ancient times.

Their cultivation most likely began in Asia about 5,000 years ago. Confucius wrote of growing roses in the Imperial Gardens about 500 B.C. and mentioned that the emperor’s library contained hundreds of books on the topic.

Red roses feature in a number of legends in ancient Greek mythology, particularly in relation to the Greek goddess of love and beauty, Aphrodite. As a result, the Romans made the rose a symbol of beauty and love — a sentiment that endures to this day.

But a rose could also mean secrecy or confidentiality. The term “sub rosa,", which literally means "under the rose," dates back to ancient times when the Romans would hang roses from the ceilings of banquet halls. It meant that what was said under the influence of wine would remain confidential.

The rose also became the symbol of the civil wars that took place in 15th century England, which would become known as the Wars of the Roses. The House of Lancaster chose the symbol of a red rose, while the House of York chose a white rose.

Many years later, the two roses were combined into one symbol. The Tudor Rose became the "Rose of England," which continues to be one of the most identifiable symbols in the UK.

WHAT ROSES MEAN IN DIFFERENT CULTURES

Today, roses are pretty much a universal symbol of love, with the meaning being shared across various cultures.

Across much of the world, the red rose in particular is associated with love and romance, which is why it is the ultimate symbol of Valentine's Day. No flower is more highly sought after than red roses on February 14th.

In Catalonia, Spain, a St. George’s Day tradition holds that people offer roses to their partners as a symbol of love. Celebrated annually on April 23rd, the day is also known as "El Dia de la Rosa" or "The Day of the Rose", and sees an exchange of roses and books between sweethearts.

Similarly in Italy, red roses symbolise love and passion and are among the most frequently-bought flowers.

Korea celebrates Rose Day on May 14th, when couples exchange fresh roses to express their love and devotion to each other.

In Japan, flowers are a traditional gift for both men and women. White roses stand for innocence, purity, and silence, while red roses are a powerful symbol of romance, love, and passion.
